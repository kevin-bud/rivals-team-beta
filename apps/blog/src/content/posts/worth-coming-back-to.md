A new screen has been added to the session. Between prompt six and the summary there is now a step headed "Anything to come back to?". Each partner can tick any of the six prompts they would like to revisit later and, if they want, add a one-line note against it. The summary that follows then opens with a "Worth coming back to" section listing those tagged prompts, with the names of whoever flagged each one and any notes underneath. The same section appears at the top of the printed PDF. Skipping the reflection entirely is allowed and produces the same summary as before.

## Why a reflection step

Six prompts ending in a summary is a record of the conversation. Six prompts ending in a chosen list of things to revisit is a conversation that gives the household something to take into the rest of the week. The summary on its own already worked — it shows what each partner said, and the household can save it. The reflection adds a small, deliberate handover: of the six things you just talked about, which are the ones you actually want to come back to.

It is the same household, on the same sofa, in the same sitting. They have just spent ten or fifteen minutes answering questions side by side. Asking them, before they close the page, which of those questions stayed with them is a small ask that does real work.

## Original prompt order, no ranking

The "Worth coming back to" list shows tagged prompts in the order they came up during the session. The tool does not score, rank, sort by perceived importance, or surface anything as "most important". If both partners tag prompt two and only one tags prompt five, prompt two appears above prompt five because two is before five — not because two is more important.

This is the same elicit-not-prescribe stance the launch post described, applied to the closing step. The household decides what is worth revisiting; the tool keeps the record.

In the launch post we said we would lean into the conversation arc rather than the plumbing. This is the tangible follow-through — a step that exists at the layer of the conversation, not the storage. Tags and notes live in the same `sessionStorage` key as the answers, no new persistence and no fetches added. The privacy posture is unchanged.
