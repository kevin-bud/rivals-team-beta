# Project Brief: Household Finance Conversation Tool

## The product

Build a web application that helps a couple or household have a productive conversation about their joint finances.

The tool is **not** a budgeting app, an accounting tool, or a financial advisor. It is something the people in a household use *together* — typically in a single sitting or recurring sessions — to surface, structure, and work through the financial topics they need to align on.

What "productive conversation" means is part of what you are deciding. Two reasonable teams could build very different products from this brief, and that is intentional.

## The user

A household of two or more adults who share some or all of their finances. Assume they are not in crisis — they are people who want to talk about money more deliberately than they currently do. They are not financial experts. They have varying levels of comfort with the topic.

## Hard constraints

1. **Web application.** Deployed and publicly accessible at a URL.
2. **Not regulated advice.** The tool must not provide personal financial, tax, legal, or investment advice. How you handle that line is your decision, but crossing it is a failure condition.
3. **Multi-user by nature.** The product must reflect that more than one person is involved. A single-user dashboard does not satisfy the brief.
4. **Privacy-respecting.** Household financial data is sensitive. Your decisions about what to store, how, and where should reflect that.

## MVP definition

Your MVP is shipped when all of the following are true:

- The application is deployed at a public URL.
- A new household can sign up or otherwise begin using the tool without manual intervention from you.
- The core interaction — whatever you have decided that is — works end-to-end for at least one realistic scenario.
- A short README at the repo root describes what the product is, who it is for, and how to use it.

After MVP, you decide what to build next.

## Public artefacts

You will publish, on a public blog associated with the project:

- A launch post when the MVP ships, explaining what you built and why.
- Release notes whenever you ship meaningful changes.
- Any other posts you think are worth writing — design decisions, things you tried and abandoned, observations about the problem space.

These posts are public. A rival team is building a competing product from the same brief and may read them. You may read theirs.

## What you control

Everything else. Stack, scope, visual design, tone, what "the conversation" actually looks like as a product, whether it is synchronous or asynchronous, structured or open-ended, opinionated or neutral, serious or playful. Make decisions and defend them in your posts.

## What you do not control

- The brief above.
- The deadline: [INSERT END TIME].
- The existence of a rival team working from the same brief in isolation from you.

## How you will be evaluated

Not on which product is "better." The evaluation is comparative and process-focused:

- What product bets did you make, and why?
- How did your decisions evolve over the project?
- Where did you and the rival diverge, and what does that suggest?
- How did you handle the regulated-advice line?
- What does your decision trail show about how you reasoned under ambiguity?

Your blog posts, commits, and internal decision log are the primary evidence.
